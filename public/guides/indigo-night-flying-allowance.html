Night Flying Allowance — Why It's Calculated From STD, Not ATD

PAH FLT Issue 01 Rev 46 §9.0 · Last verified 2 May 2026 · Reading time ~7 min

IndiGo's night flying allowance pays ₹2,000 per night hour to Captains and ₹1,000 per night hour to First Officers, prorated to the closest minute. A "night hour" is any minute of a sector that falls between 00:01 and 06:00 IST, calculated from the scheduled departure time (STD) and the Sector Value duration — not from your actual takeoff time, and not from the actual hours you flew.

The rule in one sentence

Night flying allowance is the intersection of the [STD, STD + SV-minutes] interval with the [00:01, 06:00] IST window, multiplied by ₹2,000/60 (Captain) or ₹1,000/60 (First Officer), summed across every operating sector in the month.

Senior Captain, LTC, TRI and DE roles use the Captain rate. Senior First Officers use the First Officer rate. Allowance is prorated to the closest minute, so a 35-minute night portion pays a Captain 35 × ₹33.33 = ₹1,166.

The two things that surprise pilots most

Surprise #1 — Scheduled, not actual

PAH §9.0 explicitly states the calculation is not contingent on actual hours flown and not contingent on actual time of departure. If your flight was scheduled for 02:45 IST and actually departed at 04:30, the night allowance still uses 02:45 as the basis. Conversely, a flight scheduled for 06:30 that actually took off at 05:50 earns no night allowance.

Surprise #2 — Sector Value, not actual flight time

The duration used is the Sector Value (SV) in minutes from the SV file, not the scheduled block (STA − STD) and not the actual block. SV is a published pay metric per route per UTC time slot. A 6E flight from BOM to DEL might have an SV of 126 minutes — that's the figure used, regardless of how long the flight actually took.

The night window

PAH §9.0 defines the night window as 00:01 to 06:00 IST. That's a 359-minute window. Any minute of a scheduled sector that falls inside this window — based on STD plus the SV duration — earns night allowance.

How the night portion is computed

The procedure has four steps:

  1. Take the scheduled departure time (STD in IST) for the sector.
  2. Look up the Sector Value (SV) in minutes from the SV file using the sector's flight number, route and UTC time slot.
  3. Compute the SV-arrival time: SV-arrival = STD + SV.
  4. Find the intersection of the [STD, SV-arrival] interval with the [00:01, 06:00] IST night window. The intersection length, in minutes, is the night portion.

Three patterns cover almost every real sector:

  • Sector entirely inside the night window. STD ≥ 00:01 and SV-arrival ≤ 06:00. The whole SV duration counts as night flying. Example: STD 02:45, SV 126 min → SV-arrival 04:51 → night portion = 126 min.
  • Sector starts before midnight, ends after. STD < 00:00 (i.e. the previous evening) and SV-arrival > 00:00. Only the portion from 00:01 to the SV-arrival counts. Example: STD 22:20, SV 144 min → SV-arrival 00:44 next day → night portion = 43 min (00:01 to 00:44).
  • Sector starts in the night window, ends after 06:00. STD ≥ 00:01 and SV-arrival > 06:00. Only the portion up to 06:00 counts. Example: STD 05:25, SV 180 min → SV-arrival 08:25 → night portion = 35 min (05:25 to 06:00).

Worked example

Captain C. — March, HYD base

Captain C. has three operating sectors in their March PCSR that touch the night window. (DHF positioning sectors, even at night, are not paid here — those go to the deadhead allowance.)

DateSectorSTD (IST)SV (min)SV-arrivalNight (min)Pay
06 Mar BOM → DEL 02:45 126 04:51 126 ₹4,200
15 Mar BBI → DEL 22:20 144 00:44 (+1d) 43 ₹1,433
22 Mar TRZ → DEL 05:25 180 08:25 35 ₹1,166
March night flying total = ₹4,200 + ₹1,433 + ₹1,166 = ₹6,799

Captain C. should see ₹6,799 on the "Night Flying Allowance" line of their March payslip. A First Officer flying the same three sectors would see ₹3,400.

Subtle but important. If the 06 Mar sector actually departed at 04:00 instead of the scheduled 02:45 — because of a 75-minute delay — the night portion is still 126 minutes. The actual flight was mostly post-night, but PAH §9.0 uses the schedule. Likewise, a flight scheduled at 06:15 that took off at 05:50 earns no night allowance, even though 10 minutes were actually flown in the night window.

Common reasons your night flying allowance looks wrong

1. The schedule was different from the published timetable on the day

If a flight was rescheduled before departure, the new STD becomes the basis. CrewAllowance.com pulls the operative scheduled time from FlightAware AeroAPI for the date of operation. If your night allowance looks lower than expected, it may be that the schedule was advanced or pushed back from the version you remember.

2. The Sector Value file isn't out yet (early-month re-runs)

The SV file is published around the 20th–21st of the following month. If you run the calculator before then, the night flying figure may be a fallback estimate using the previous month's SV — typically very close, but not exact. Re-run after the SV file lands for the canonical figure.

3. A DHF sector flew through the night

DHF (deadhead-forward positioning) sectors do not pay night flying allowance, even if every minute is in the night window. The allowance for those sectors is captured under deadhead allowance (PAH §1.0) instead. If a positioning sector through the night appears under your night flying breakdown, the DHF flag may have been missed in PCSR parsing.

4. Confusion with night-time duty pay or fatigue rest

Night flying allowance under PAH §9.0 is distinct from any night-time duty allowance, fatigue rest provisions or roster constraints. It is paid purely on the night-portion of operating sectors — nothing else. If your payslip has additional night-related figures, they belong to other PAH sections.

5. The SV time-slot lookup hit the wrong row

The SV file is keyed on flight number, departure airport, arrival airport and a UTC time slot. If a flight has multiple SV rows for different time slots, the calculation must use the one matching the STD's UTC slot. CrewAllowance.com does this match exactly; manual spreadsheets sometimes use the first row found and produce a slightly different SV figure.

Frequently asked questions

What is the night flying allowance for IndiGo pilots?

IndiGo's night flying allowance under PAH §9.0 pays ₹2,000 per night hour to Captains and ₹1,000 per night hour to First Officers, prorated to the closest minute. A "night hour" is any minute of a sector that falls between 00:01 and 06:00 IST, calculated from the scheduled departure time (STD) and the Sector Value (SV) duration — not from actual takeoff or actual flight time.

Why is night flying allowance based on STD instead of actual departure (ATD)?

PAH §9.0 explicitly states that night flying is calculated from the scheduled departure time (STD), not the actual departure (ATD). The reasoning is fairness: a pilot scheduled to fly through the night should not lose allowance because the flight was delayed past the night window, and conversely should not gain allowance because the flight took off early. The schedule is the basis; the actual operation does not change the night-hour count.

What time window counts as 'night' for IndiGo night flying allowance?

PAH §9.0 defines the night window as 00:01 to 06:00 IST. That's a 359-minute window. Any minute of a sector that falls inside this window — based on STD plus the SV duration — earns night allowance. A sector that crosses midnight has its night portion counted starting from 00:01.

How is the night portion of a sector calculated?

Take the scheduled departure time (STD in IST). Add the Sector Value (SV) duration in minutes — this gives the SV-arrival time. The night portion is the intersection of the [STD, SV-arrival] interval with the [00:01, 06:00] IST window. For example, an STD of 02:45 with a 126-minute SV gives an SV-arrival of 04:51, entirely within the night window — pay is 126 minutes × ₹2,000/60 = ₹4,200 for a Captain.

Does night flying apply to deadhead (DHF) sectors?

No. Night flying allowance under PAH §9.0 applies only to operating sectors. DHF (deadhead-forward) sectors are paid under the deadhead allowance (PAH §1.0) instead. If a pilot is non-operating crew on a sector that flies through the night, that sector contributes to deadhead pay, not night flying pay.

Why does my night flying allowance differ from the night hours I actually flew?

PAH §9.0 is not contingent on actual hours flown or actual takeoff time. It uses scheduled departure and Sector Value duration. If your flight was delayed by 90 minutes and you actually flew most of it after 06:00 IST, the calculation still uses the schedule — and the night portion remains based on what would have happened on time. Conversely, if a delay pushed your operation deeper into the night, the allowance does not increase.

Where does the SV (Sector Value) figure come from?

The Sector Value file is published monthly by IndiGo via 6eBreeze (Departments – OCC – Sector Pay), typically released around the 20th–21st of the following month. Each sector has an SV in minutes, indexed by flight number, departure airport, arrival airport and a UTC time slot. CrewAllowance.com matches each sector in your PCSR against the SV file to obtain the SV duration for night flying calculations.

What if my SV file isn't out yet at the time I upload my PCSR?

If you run the calculator before the SV file is published (typically before the 21st of the month following the operation), the calculator will fall back to the previous month's SV file and surface a banner noting the approximation. Re-run once the canonical SV is uploaded for the exact night flying figure.
