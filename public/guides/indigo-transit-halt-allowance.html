Transit Halt Allowance — The 90-Minute Rule and 15-Minute Diff

PAH FLT Issue 01 Rev 46 §7.0 · Last verified 2 May 2026 · Reading time ~8 min

The transit halt allowance pays ₹1,000 per hour to Captains and ₹500 per hour to First Officers, prorated to the closest minute and capped at 4 hours per halt (₹4,000 / ₹2,000 maximum). It applies to halts within a duty period — typically between two consecutive operating sectors — that meet either the scheduled-≥-90-minute rule or the actual-≥-90-minute-AND-diff->-15-minute exception.

The rule in one sentence

For every consecutive sector pair within the same duty, if the halt is long enough under either eligibility rule, pay ₹1,000/hr (Captain) or ₹500/hr (First Officer) on the appropriate basis (scheduled or actual), capped at 4 hours.

The rate (effective 1 January 2026)

RankPer hourPer minuteCap per halt
Captain₹1,000₹16.67₹4,000 (240 min)
First Officer₹500₹8.33₹2,000 (240 min)

Senior Captain, LTC, TRI and DE roles use the Captain rate. Senior First Officers use the First Officer rate. The cap means that a 5-hour halt and a 4-hour halt pay the same.

Eligibility — the 90-minute rule (with the 15-minute exception)

A halt qualifies for transit allowance if either of two conditions is true:

Condition (i) — scheduled halt ≥ 90 minutes

If the scheduled time between chocks-on of the inbound and chocks-off of the outbound is at least 90 minutes, the halt is eligible. The actual operation does not change this — even a delayed early-arrival or push-back doesn't disqualify a halt that was scheduled long.

Condition (ii) — actual halt ≥ 90 min AND |actual − scheduled| > 15 min

A halt that was scheduled under 90 minutes can still qualify if the actual halt ran to 90 minutes or more, AND the difference between actual and scheduled exceeded 15 minutes. This catches genuine delays that turned a quick turn into a long one — the schedule said 70 minutes, the inbound was 30 minutes late, and you ended up sitting at the airport for 100 minutes.

If neither condition is met, the halt does not pay. A halt scheduled at 80 minutes that ran to 95 minutes (diff = 15 minutes, not strictly greater than) does not qualify under condition (ii) — the diff must be more than 15 minutes.

Payment basis — scheduled or actual?

Once a halt is eligible, the payment is computed on either the scheduled or the actual halt:

  • If |actual − scheduled| ≤ 15 minutes, the pay basis is the scheduled halt.
  • If |actual − scheduled| > 15 minutes, the pay basis is the actual halt.

Then apply the cap: if the chosen basis exceeds 240 minutes, the pay-minutes are clipped to 240. Multiply by ₹1,000/60 (Captain) or ₹500/60 (First Officer), prorated to the closest minute.

The unique-knowledge bit — home-base transit halts COUNT

Many pilots assume transit allowance excludes the home base — it doesn't. PAH §7.0 has no home-base exclusion. A 90-minute transit at your own base between two operating sectors of the same duty is just as eligible as one at any outstation. The largest single transit halts in a typical month are often at the pilot's home base — don't leave that money on the table.

Worked example

Worth noting: the two largest payments (₹1,966 and ₹2,333) are both at DEL — Captain D.'s home base. That's the home-base-counts rule in action.

Common reasons your transit allowance looks wrong

1. Home-base halts were excluded by mistake

The single most common error. A 90-minute halt at your home base between two operating sectors is fully eligible. If your transit total looks light and you have any home-base halts in the month, this is the first place to check.

2. The 15-minute boundary was applied as ≥ instead of >

PAH §7.0's condition (ii) requires the diff to be strictly greater than 15 minutes — exactly 15 minutes does not qualify under (ii). A halt scheduled at 80 minutes that ran to 95 minutes (diff = 15) does not pay. CrewAllowance.com applies this strictly.

3. A DHT sector on either side of the halt was missed

Halts involving any DHT sector — either the inbound or the outbound leg — are excluded under PAH §7.0. DHF sectors are not excluded, only DHT. If your PCSR shows a DHT flag in the Other Crew section that wasn't picked up by the calculator, an excluded halt may have been counted in error.

4. Different duty periods were treated as one

Transit applies only to halts within a single duty period — typically with a gap under 8 hours between sectors. A break between separate duties is a layover (under PAH §2.0) or a rest period, not a transit halt. CrewAllowance.com uses the duty boundaries from your PCSR to keep the two cleanly separated.

5. Delays before the first flight of the day were counted

A delay before your first flight of the day is not a transit halt — there's no inbound leg to halt from. Only halts between two flown sectors qualify.

6. International transits were silently included

CrewAllowance.com excludes non-domestic halts from the transit calculation. If your transit line is unexpectedly large after an international rotation, the foreign portion may have leaked in — flag it.

Frequently asked questions

What is the IndiGo transit halt allowance?

IndiGo's transit halt allowance under PAH §7.0 pays ₹1,000 per hour to Captains and ₹500 per hour to First Officers, prorated to the closest minute and capped at 4 hours per halt (₹4,000 / ₹2,000 maximum). It applies to halts within a duty period — typically between two consecutive operating sectors — that meet the 90-minute eligibility rule.

What is the 90-minute rule for transit halt allowance?

A halt qualifies under PAH §7.0 if either (i) the scheduled halt is at least 90 minutes, or (ii) the actual halt is at least 90 minutes AND the difference between actual and scheduled exceeds 15 minutes. If neither condition is met, the halt does not pay.

Is transit halt allowance based on scheduled or actual halt time?

It depends on how close the actual halt was to the schedule. If |actual − scheduled| ≤ 15 minutes, the payment is based on the scheduled halt. If |actual − scheduled| > 15 minutes, the payment is based on the actual halt. The 15-minute tolerance prevents small variations from changing pay; longer delays are recognised by paying the longer actual halt.

Does transit halt allowance apply at my home base?

Yes. Unlike TLPD (PAH §2.0), the transit halt allowance has no home-base exclusion. PAH §7.0 simply pays for any qualifying halt within a duty period, regardless of station. A 90-minute transit at your home base between two operating sectors of the same duty is just as eligible as one at any outstation.

What is the maximum transit halt allowance per halt?

The cap under PAH §7.0 is 4 hours per halt — ₹4,000 for Captains, ₹2,000 for First Officers. Any halt longer than 4 hours pays only the cap; the excess time over 4 hours does not earn additional allowance.

When is a halt not eligible for transit allowance?

A halt does not qualify if both: (a) the scheduled halt is under 90 minutes, AND (b) either the actual halt is also under 90 minutes, or the difference between actual and scheduled is 15 minutes or less. PAH §7.0 also excludes any halt that involves a DHT sector on either side, and delays before the first flight of the day do not count.

Do DHF sectors disqualify a transit halt?

No — only DHT sectors disqualify a halt. PAH §7.0 explicitly allows halts where one side (or both sides) is a DHF sector, as long as no DHT sector is involved.

Are transit halts at international stations paid the same way?

No. CrewAllowance.com excludes non-domestic halts from the transit allowance calculation. International transits are governed by separate PAH provisions and do not appear on the domestic transit halt line of your payslip.
