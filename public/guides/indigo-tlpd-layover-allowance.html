TLPD on Your IndiGo Payslip — Domestic Layover Allowance Explained

PAH FLT Issue 01 Rev 46 §2.0 · Last verified 2 May 2026 · Reading time ~7 min

TLPD is the domestic layover allowance under PAH §2.0. It pays a flat ₹3,000 (Captain) or ₹1,500 (First Officer) for any layover at a station away from your home base lasting between 10 hours 1 minute and 24 hours, plus ₹150/hour (Captain) or ₹75/hour (First Officer) rounded up for every hour beyond 24. Eligibility is measured from actual chocks-on at the outstation to actual chocks-off when you depart it.

The rule in one sentence

TLPD pays a flat base for layovers in the 10:01–24:00 hour band, then accrues hourly beyond 24 hours, but only when the layover is at a station other than your registered home base.

The rate (effective 1 January 2026)

Layover duration Captain First Officer
0:00 – 10:00 hrs Not eligible Not eligible
10:01 – 24:00 hrs ₹3,000 flat ₹1,500 flat
Beyond 24:00 hrs ₹3,000 + ₹150/hr (round up) ₹1,500 + ₹75/hr (round up)

Senior Captain, LTC, TRI and DE roles use the Captain rate. Senior First Officers use the First Officer rate. Each hour beyond 24 is rounded up to the next full hour — so 24:01 to 25:00 counts as one hour, 25:01 to 26:00 counts as two, and so on.

Eligibility — when TLPD is paid

For a layover to qualify under PAH §2.0, three conditions must all be true:

  • The layover station is not your home base. A long break at your registered home base after a duty is not a layover for TLPD purposes — it is a return-to-base.
  • The duration is at least 10 hours and 1 minute. A 9-hour-59-minute hotel break does not qualify, however inconvenient. The threshold is set in PAH §2.0 and is not pro-rated.
  • The layover is domestic. International layovers are governed by PAH §8.2 (foreign layover allowance) and do not appear in TLPD on your payslip.
Don't confuse with transit halt. A halt within the same duty period (typically 90 minutes to 4 hours, no hotel involved) is paid under PAH §7.0 — see Transit Halt Allowance. TLPD and transit halt are mutually exclusive: a single break never pays both.

How the duration is measured

PAH §2.0 measures the layover from actual chocks-on at the outstation (the inbound aircraft's ATA, when the wheels are chocked on stand) to actual chocks-off when the next aircraft departs the outstation (the outbound ATD). This includes:

  • The post-flight checks and walk to the airport exit at the outstation.
  • Ground transfer from the airport to the hotel.
  • Hotel rest.
  • Ground transfer from the hotel back to the airport.
  • Pre-flight duties up to chocks-off on the outbound sector.

This is why the eligible duration is typically longer than the time you actually spent inside the hotel room. If your hotel stay was 14 hours but the chocks-on-to-chocks-off window was 17 hours 30 minutes, the layover is treated as 17:30 for TLPD purposes — and falls inside the 10:01–24:00 band, paying ₹3,000.

Worked example

Captain B. — March, BOM base

Captain B. has three layovers in their March PCSR:

  1. HYD layover, 6–7 March. Chocks-on at HYD on 6 Mar at 22:10 IST. Chocks-off at HYD on 7 Mar at 14:25 IST. Duration: 16 h 15 min — falls in the 10:01–24:00 band. Pay: ₹3,000.
  2. BLR layover, 14–15 March. Chocks-on at BLR on 14 Mar at 19:42 IST. Chocks-off at BLR on 15 Mar at 06:05 IST. Duration: 10 h 23 min — just over the 10:01 threshold. Pay: ₹3,000.
  3. DEL extended layover, 22–24 March. Chocks-on at DEL on 22 Mar at 23:10 IST. Chocks-off at DEL on 24 Mar at 07:35 IST. Duration: 32 h 25 min — 24:00 of base + 8 h 25 min beyond. The "beyond" portion rounds up to 9 hours. Pay: ₹3,000 + (9 × ₹150) = ₹4,350.
March TLPD total = ₹3,000 + ₹3,000 + ₹4,350 = ₹10,350

Captain B. should see ₹10,350 on the "TLPD" line of their March payslip. A First Officer flying the same three layovers would see ₹5,175.

The spill-over rule (the most-missed part of PAH §2.0)

A layover that begins on the last day of one month and concludes in the next month is paid in full in the month it concludes — not split across the two payslips. The duration cannot be measured until chocks-off, so the airline cannot pay any portion until the layover is complete.

Captain B. continued — 31 March → 1 April CCU layover

Captain B.'s last duty of March ends with a CCU layover:

  • Chocks-on at CCU on 31 March at 21:50 IST.
  • Chocks-off at CCU on 1 April at 13:10 IST.
  • Duration: 15 h 20 min — falls in the 10:01–24:00 band, ₹3,000.

This ₹3,000 appears on the April payslip, not March. It does not appear in Captain B.'s March TLPD total of ₹10,350 above. If you check your March payslip and the TLPD figure looks short by exactly one layover, this is almost always why.

Common reasons the TLPD on your payslip looks wrong

1. A spill-over layover from last month is being attributed to the wrong month

The most common single discrepancy. A layover that began on the last day of a month and ended on the first of the next is paid in the concluding month. Cross-check your previous month's payslip and your current month's PCSR before flagging the difference.

2. A home-base "layover" was paid by mistake (or excluded by mistake)

If your roster shows a long break at your home base and you saw TLPD for it on your payslip, that's an overpayment. Conversely, if the airline mis-coded your home base in the system and started excluding genuine outstation layovers, you'll be underpaid. Always verify the home base recorded in your profile matches the IATA code of your roster base.

3. The "beyond 24 hours" rounding was applied incorrectly

Hours beyond 24 are rounded up to the next full hour, not down or to the nearest. A 32 h 25 min layover gives 9 hours of rounding-up (8:25 rounds up to 9), not 8. If your TLPD looks ₹150 short on a long layover, it's likely a rounding error.

4. Same-station back-to-back layovers were split when they should have been treated as one

If you arrived at an outstation, did a there-and-back duty without crossing your home base, and returned to the same outstation, the two halves can sometimes be treated as a single continuous layover. The decision turns on whether the intervening duty visited the home base or not. CrewAllowance.com applies the same coalescing logic the airline uses.

5. International layovers were silently included

International stations (SIN, DOH, AUH, DAC, etc.) are governed by PAH §8.2 (foreign layover allowance) and should not appear under TLPD. If you flew an international rotation last month and your TLPD line is unexpectedly large, the foreign portion may have leaked in.

Frequently asked questions

What does TLPD mean on an IndiGo pilot payslip?

TLPD is the domestic layover allowance under PAH §2.0. It is paid when an IndiGo cockpit crew member spends more than 10 hours 1 minute on a layover at any station that is not their home base. The flat rate is ₹3,000 (Captain) or ₹1,500 (First Officer) for layovers between 10:01 and 24:00 hours, plus ₹150/hour (Captain) or ₹75/hour (First Officer) rounded up for any time beyond 24 hours.

How is the IndiGo domestic layover allowance calculated?

PAH §2.0 measures the layover from actual chocks-on at the outstation to actual chocks-off when departing it. For 10:01 to 24:00 hours, a flat ₹3,000 is paid (Captain). Beyond 24 hours, ₹150/hour is added on top, with each hour rounded up. First Officer rates are exactly half the Captain rates.

Does TLPD apply at my home base?

No. PAH §2.0 explicitly limits the domestic layover allowance to layovers away from the pilot's home base. A long break at home base after a duty is regarded as a return-to-base, not a layover. (Transit halts under PAH §7.0 are different — they are payable even at the home base.)

What is the spill-over rule for layovers across the month-end?

When a layover begins on the last calendar day of a month and concludes in the next month, the entire layover is paid in the month it ends. So a layover starting 31 March and ending 1 April will appear on the April payslip, not March. The duration cannot be measured until chocks-off is known.

Is TLPD the same as the transit halt allowance?

No. TLPD (PAH §2.0) is for overnight or extended stays away from home base where the crew member is rostered with hotel and ground transport. The transit halt allowance (PAH §7.0) covers halts within a duty period (typically 90 minutes to 4 hours) where the crew stays at the airport. The two are mutually exclusive.

Why is my TLPD different from the time I actually spent at the hotel?

TLPD is measured from actual chocks-on at the outstation to actual chocks-off when departing — not from hotel check-in to check-out. Ground transfers between airport and hotel, post-flight checks, and pre-flight duties all count. The eligible duration is typically longer than the time you spent in the hotel room.

Are international layovers paid the same way?

No. International layovers are calculated under PAH §8.2 (foreign layover allowance), not §2.0, and do not appear in TLPD on your payslip. CrewAllowance.com flags international layovers in your breakdown but does not currently compute the foreign allowance.

What if my outbound flight is delayed and the layover unexpectedly crosses the 24-hour mark?

The duration is measured from actual chocks-on to actual chocks-off, so a delay that pushes the layover beyond 24 hours does qualify for the additional ₹150/hour (Captain) or ₹75/hour (First Officer). The rounding-up rule means even a 24:01 layover triggers one full hour of additional allowance.
